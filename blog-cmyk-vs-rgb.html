CMYK VS RGB

Why your printed colours don't match your screen, and how to stop it happening.

By Aura Print & Promo · July 2026 · 3 minute read

It's the most common colour conversation in printing: "It looked brighter on my screen." It did. Here's why, and what to do about it.

Two completely different ways of making colour

RGB (Red, Green, Blue) is how screens work. A monitor starts black and adds light. Mix all three at full strength and you get white. Because it's built from glowing light, RGB can produce intensely vivid colours - electric blues, neon greens, hot oranges.

CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black) is how presses work. Paper starts white and ink subtracts light from it. Mix more ink, get darker colour. There's no glow in a sheet of paper, so the most saturated RGB colours physically cannot be reproduced in ink.

What happens when RGB goes to print

If you supply an RGB file, it has to be converted to CMYK before it can be printed. Colours inside the shared range come through fine. Colours outside it get pulled to the nearest printable equivalent - which is why that electric blue turns slightly purple and that vivid green goes a touch dull. The conversion is automatic, but the result can drift from what you expected.

The fix: design in CMYK from the start, or convert to CMYK yourself before exporting. That way, what you see in your design program is much closer to what comes off the press - and any colour compromise is your call, not the software's.

When colour really matters

Brand colours that must match exactly - across business cards, signage, vehicle magnets and everything else - are best specified properly:

  • PMS (Pantone) codes - the universal standard for exact colour matching.
  • A CMYK breakdown - the exact ink percentages for your brand colours.
  • A printed sample - give us something previously printed and we'll match to it, accounting for stock and finish.

The rich black trick

One CMYK quirk worth knowing: large areas of plain 100% black ink often print grey and patchy. The fix is "rich black" - a mix of all four inks (C:60 M:40 Y:40 K:100) that produces a deep, even black. Use rich black for backgrounds and large fills, and plain 100% K for small text.

Check your proof

Every job we print gets a proof before production. Remember you'll be viewing that proof on a screen - in RGB - so use it to check content and layout, and trust CMYK values and printed samples for colour. If a colour is critical, tell us and we'll work with you to get it right.
